Sum of exponential and laplace distributions

Craig Cahillane
June 16, 2020

I proved the following result while working on my thesis. The result turned out to be not relevant to my thesis, but I wanted to record the result here since working through it was instructive.

Suppose we have two independent random variables 𝒳∼Exp⁢(λ) and 𝒴∼Laplace(μ=0,γ). Let 𝒵=𝒳+𝒴. What is the probability density function f𝒵⁢(z) of 𝒵?

Exp⁢(λ) is the exponential distribution, defined such that λ is its mean:

Exp⁢(λ)=f𝒳⁢(x)=1λ⁢e-xλ  x∈[0,∞) (1)

where f𝒳 is the probability density function (PDF), and x is the parameter of 𝒳.

Laplace(μ=0,γ) is the laplace distribution, with its mean μ defined to be zero and scale parameter γ:

Laplace⁢(μ,γ)=f𝒴⁢(y)=12⁢γ⁢e-|y-μ|γ  y∈(∞,∞) (2)

Since 𝒳,𝒴 are independent, we can define the joint probability distribution f𝒳⁢𝒴⁢(x,y) as the product of the two PDFs:

f𝒳⁢𝒴⁢(x,y) =f𝒳⁢(x)⁢f𝒴⁢(y) (3)
=12⁢λ⁢γ⁢e-xλ⁢e-|y|γ  x∈[0,∞) y∈(∞,∞) (4)

We will start with the cumulative distribution function (CDF) F𝒵⁢(z). The CDF is defined as the probability that the random variable 𝒵 takes a value that is less than z: F𝒵(z)=P(𝒵<z). Since as z→∞ all values must be less than z, F𝒵⁢(z)→1, and we can write F𝒵(z)=1-P(𝒵≥z). Recall that the PDF is equal to the derivative of the CDF: f𝒵=d⁢F𝒵d⁢z.

We split the solution into parts. The first part is finding F𝒵⁢(z) for z<0. The only way for z=x+y<0 to be less than zero is if y<-x, because x∈[0,∞). Also, since we know y<0, we can write |y|=-y for the joint probability distribution. Thus we have

F𝒵(z<0) =P(x+y<z)
=P(y<z-x)
=∫0∞𝑑x⁢∫-∞z-x𝑑y⁢f𝒳⁢𝒴⁢(x,y)
=12⁢λ⁢γ⁢∫0∞𝑑x⁢∫-∞z-x𝑑y⁢e-xλ⁢eyγ
=ezγ2⁢λ⁢∫0∞𝑑x⁢e-x⁢(λ+γ)γ⁢λ
=γ⁢ezγ2⁢(λ+γ)  z∈[0,∞) (5)

This concludes the first part, z<0.

The second part is finding F𝒵⁢(z) for z>0.

F𝒵(z>0) =P(x+y<z)
=1-P(x+y>z)
=1-P(y>z-x)
=1-12⁢λ⁢γ⁢∫0∞𝑑x⁢∫z-x∞𝑑y⁢e-xλ⁢e|y|γ (6)

There are two ways for z=x+y>0: y<0 but x>-y, and simply y>0. The difficultly lies in the change in PDF f𝒴 at y=0. We will split this integral into two more parts: x>z and x<z, at which point we will switch between PDFs.

First, let x>z. Then we have the sum of two integrals, one with f𝒴(y<0) and the other with f𝒴(y>0):

P(y>z-x|x>z) =12⁢λ⁢γ⁢∫z∞𝑑x⁢∫z-x∞𝑑y⁢e-xλ⁢e|y|γ
=12⁢λ⁢γ⁢∫z∞𝑑x⁢[∫z-x0𝑑y⁢e-xλ⁢eyγ+∫0∞𝑑y⁢e-xλ⁢e-yγ]
=12⁢λ⁢∫z∞𝑑x⁢[e-x⁢(1γ+1λ)⁢(ex/γ-ez/γ)+e-xλ]
=12⁢λ⁢∫z∞𝑑x⁢[e-x⁢(1γ+1λ)⁢(2⁢ex/γ-ez/γ)]
=(γ+2⁢λ)⁢e-zλ2⁢(γ+λ) (8)

Second, let x<z. Now for z>x+y, y>0, so we can just consider f𝒴(y>0):

P(y>z-x|x<z) =12⁢λ⁢γ⁢∫0z𝑑x⁢∫z-x∞𝑑y⁢e-xλ⁢e|y|γ
=12⁢λ⁢γ⁢∫0z𝑑x⁢∫z-x∞𝑑y⁢e-xλ⁢e-yγ
=12⁢λ⁢∫0z𝑑x⁢exγ-xλ-zγ
=γ⁢(e-zγ-e-zλ)2⁢(γ-λ) (9)

Summing Eqs. Sum of exponential and laplace distributions and Sum of exponential and laplace distributions:

F𝒵(z>0) =1-P(y>z-x)
=1-P(y>z-x|x>z)+P(y>z-x|x<z)
=1-(γ+2⁢λ)⁢e-zλ2⁢(γ+λ)-γ⁢(e-zγ-e-zλ)2⁢(γ-λ)
=1+λ2⁢e-zλγ2-λ2-γ⁢e-zγ2⁢(γ-λ)  z∈[0,∞] (10)

This concludes the second part, z>0.

Thus the total CDF is given by

F𝒵⁢(z)={γ⁢ezγ2⁢(λ+γ)z<01+λ2⁢e-zλγ2-λ2-γ⁢e-zγ2⁢(γ-λ)z≥0 (11)

Taking the derivative of 11 gives the PDF f𝒵⁢(z):

f𝒵⁢(z)={ezγ2⁢(λ+γ)z<0λ⁢e-zλλ2-γ2+e-zγ2⁢γ-2⁢λz≥0 (12)
